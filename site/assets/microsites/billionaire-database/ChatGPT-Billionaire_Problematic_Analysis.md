Third, his public footprint stays mostly in those lanes (foundation, big gifts, local impact) rather than interviews or public persona building, which is its own kind of power move. ([Sun Family Foundation][43])

James Leprino (1937–2025; “cheese king” wealth estimated around $8.2B in mid-2025 by Forbes). ([Forbes][45])

([Reuters][1])  The U.S. Treasury has called him an arms dealer and “financial henchman” of a repressive junta, and later described him as owner of companies providing equipment/services including arms to the Burmese military. ([U.S. Department of the Treasury][2])  Reuters also quotes the “regime’s top crony” framing and reports him boasting that sanctions made him richer and the generals stronger, which is basically moustache-twirl dialogue. ([Reuters][1])  Control levers are the classic crony kit: privileged licences/contracts + a business network that can move money and import restricted stuff (even banking licences were tied up with military-linked tycoons). ([Reuters][1])

Dan Gertler (Israel/DRC mining). His wealth story is “be the tollbooth”: U.S. Treasury says he used a close friendship with then-president Joseph Kabila to act as a middleman for mining asset sales, to the point that multinationals “had to go through” him to do business with the Congolese state. ([U.S. Department of the Treasury][3])  Treasury also says he and his company used offshore companies to facilitate deals and frames his fortune as coming from “opaque and corrupt” mining and oil deals. ([U.S. Department of the Treasury][3])  Reuters and the FT describe the sanctions/waiver drama and the way the system still bends around him (waivers, proposed relief tied to him exiting Congo, continuing royalty flows). ([Reuters][4])  “Who is he” in public record is basically: sanctioned billionaire, politically-networked fixer, and a guy who fights it out through lobbying/lawyers while denying wrongdoing. ([U.S. Department of the Treasury][5])

Arkady & Boris Rotenberg (Russia). U.S. Treasury sanctioned them in 2014 and flat-out described the model: support Putin’s projects, receive “high price contracts” from Gazprom and Sochi Olympics work, and make billions during Putin’s rule. ([U.S. Department of the Treasury][6])  Another control lever Treasury highlights is asset reshuffling inside the family after sanctions (e.g., selling major stakes to Arkady’s son Igor after designation). ([U.S. Department of the Treasury][7])  For “what type of person”: Reuters reports Arkady stepping up to claim ownership of the palace Navalny’s team linked to Putin, in a classic “take the heat for the boss” move. ([Reuters][8])  There’s also the sanctions-evasion culture around them: a U.S. Senate investigation digs into how sanctioned Russian oligarchs used the opaque art market, and Rotenberg-linked structures are part of that storyline. ([hsgac.senate.gov][9])  The UK’s own sanctions notice language is blunt about being “involved” in destabilising Ukraine / undermining sovereignty. ([GOV.UK][10])

Isabel dos Santos (Angola). This one is “elite-daughter capitalism” with prosecutors and sanctions attached: Reuters says the UK imposed a travel ban + asset freeze on her under its anti-corruption regime; she denies wrongdoing and calls it political. ([Reuters][11])  Reuters also reports UK court action freezing assets tied to a Unitel dispute and her losing an appeal against the freezing order. ([Reuters][12])  For the “who is she” layer, Time’s write-up of the Luanda Leaks frames the accusation as using influence and shell companies to build a cross-border empire, including a Sonangol-linked money transfer allegation; again she denies. ([TIME][13])  And ICIJ’s coverage of the UK sanctions literally uses the “stealing country’s wealth for personal gain” framing (that’s their summary of the government’s rationale, not a proven fact). ([icij.org][14])  Control levers here are board/command positions in state-adjacent giants plus international corporate plumbing that makes assets hard to grab. ([TIME][13])

The Gupta brothers (South Africa / Dubai). If you want “cartoon villain but real”, “state capture” is basically the genre: South Africa’s own State Capture Commission explainer points straight at allegations the Guptas essentially “captured” the state (originating with the Public Protector’s report). ([SONA 2026][15])  The IMF’s summary of the evidence presented to the Zondo Commission describes a systematic plan with the Gupta family to capture key state-owned entities via appointments. ([PFM][16])  Reuters reports Interpol red notices relating to suspected fraud and notes the Guptas’ denials. ([Reuters][17])  The Guardian’s reporting summarises the broader public allegation set: bribery for contracts and influence over ministerial appointments during the Zuma era, then fleeing to Dubai. ([The Guardian][18])  Wealth/control methods are basically: political placement + procurement extraction + cross-border escape velocity. ([PFM][16])

Joseph Lau (Hong Kong). This is “luxury-villain energy” plus an actual conviction: Reuters reports he was found guilty in Macau of bribery and money laundering in a land deal and notes the practical reality that, without an extradition treaty, he could avoid imprisonment unless he travelled into the wrong jurisdiction. ([Reuters][19])  Personality-wise he’s the guy who buys record diamonds and names them after his daughter (Reuters on “Star of Josephine”; other coverage on later mega-purchases). ([Reuters][20])  Control levers: real-estate wealth + political/land access games + jurisdictional arbitrage. ([Reuters][19])

Rami Makhlouf (Syria). U.S. Treasury designated him back in 2008, describing him as a regime-linked businessman who built an empire by exploiting relationships, manipulating the judicial system, and using intelligence officials to intimidate rivals while pursuing exclusive licences and contract awards. ([U.S. Department of the Treasury][21])  Later Treasury materials underline the “regime insider improperly benefitting” framing. ([U.S. Department of the Treasury][22])  The Guardian’s profile of his feud with Assad is revealing because it treats him as the country’s richest man and lays out how deeply his businesses sat inside the state and war economy (including the Syriatel fight and staff arrests). ([The Guardian][23])  If you want a clean “type of person” read: sanctioned kleptocrat-archetype, then a public family meltdown when the protection racket turns inward. ([U.S. Department of the Treasury][21])
