Brexit: a Trillion pounds and counting…

Brexit isn’t a one-off bill. It’s a persistent drag: the economy is smaller than the no‑Brexit baseline, every year, and the gap compounds. That’s how you get to a trillion without a single dramatic moment — just slow leak, plus time.

This isn’t money we might lose. This is money we already lost — and we’re not even trying to scrape it back.

Where the cost shows up (and why it feels like “no money”)

The “tab” isn’t a pile of banknotes in Brussels. It’s deadweight loss, lower investment, and a bunch of friction that turns normal economic activity into overhead. Some of the leak is obvious; some of it is political opportunity.

  • Real-income hit: sterling drop → higher import prices → squeezed living standards.
  • Investment hole: less kit, less productivity, weaker future wages.
  • Trade/admin friction: paperwork and border processes as a permanent tax on SMEs and exporters.
  • Leakage: exception‑politics procurement and chaos‑as‑invoices dynamics (harder to measure cleanly, but the mechanism is real).

The chart is a deliberately simple “shape of the problem” model: annual impacts plus a cumulative line that crosses the psychological threshold and then keeps climbing.
