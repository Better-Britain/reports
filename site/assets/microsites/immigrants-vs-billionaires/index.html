Consumer detriment categories below are an illustrative split of the NatCen total (£71.2Bn), so you can test assumptions.

Gains

CAF's £7.96Bn estimate is for the wealthiest 1% (millionaires), not billionaires specifically. This model uses a generous billionaire-attributed estimate of 50%: ~£4.0Bn.

Innovation is included here only as an explicit scenario toggle. The attribution problem is severe: it is hard to isolate innovation caused by billionaire ownership versus public R&D, workers, and wider ecosystem effects. The £6.0Bn figure is an optimistic heuristic from UK business R&D spend: ONS reports £55.6Bn in 2024, and this model assumes roughly 10.8% as a speculative attributable share (55.6 × 0.108 ≈ 6.0).

Immigration assumptions

Selected costs also reduce selected benefits proportionally in this model to represent underinvestment drag. This includes both regular and irregular migration costs.

Costs

Why the lower end is around a quarter-million: ONS’s latest provisional estimate puts UK long-term net migration at 204,000 in YE June 2025, down from 649,000 a year earlier. That drop is huge. The estimates are provisional and revised over time, so yes, there can be a bit of numberwangery in the first pass, but it is still a very sharp fall.

Immigrants aren't expensive, they're a great deal

If you’re blaming immigrants because you think they’re “expensive”, you’ve been sold the wrong villain. On official-style forecasting, immigration mostly looks like a return. The big, boring drain is extraction and leakage at the top.

Numbers

This is a rough “what’s the order of magnitude?” model, not a perfect economic forecast. Each line below says what number we used, why we used it, and where it came from.

  • “Billionaires” cost (~£80Bn): NatCen’s estimate of UK consumer detriment (£71.2Bn; money people lose from bad markets/products) plus a selected slice of HMRC’s tax gap (£46.8Bn total; using the large business + wealthy shares) to give about £8.0Bn on top. The consumer split in the toggle panel is for scenario testing only.
  • Immigration cost (~£12Bn): parliamentary evidence on asylum accommodation/support spend (~£4Bn in 2024–25) plus OBR’s estimate of extra departmental spend needed to keep per-person services steady: ~£8.1Bn DEL adjustment by 2028–29.
  • Immigration benefit (~£47.5Bn): OBR says GDP is about +1.5% higher by 2028–29 in the higher-migration scenario. Applying that to UK cash GDP (£2,891Bn in 2024) gives ~£43.4Bn, then we add ~£4.1Bn/year migration fees/charges. In the immigration assumptions panel, selected costs reduce selected benefits by the same proportion as a demonstrative underinvestment drag assumption.
  • “Billionaires” philanthropy estimate (~£4.0Bn): CAF/Altrata reports high-value giving of £7.96Bn in 2023 (for the wealthiest 1%) for the wealthiest 1% (millionaires, not billionaires only). This model applies a generous 50% billionaire-attributed share as a heuristic.
  • Tax contribution scenario (+£1.6Bn, optional toggle): PwC reports the UK 100 Group paid £31Bn in taxes borne (big-company contribution estimate). This model uses a conservative 5% attributable share for billionaire-led firms (31 × 0.05 ≈ 1.6) as an arguable upside line.
  • Innovation scenario (+£6Bn, optional toggle) = optimistic heuristic: 10.8% of UK business R&D spend £55.6Bn UK business R&D spend (ONS, 2024) ≈ £6.0Bn. This is a scenario assumption, not a measured “billionaire-caused” estimate.
